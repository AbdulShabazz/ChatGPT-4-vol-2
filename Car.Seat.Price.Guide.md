* Budget: $40–$80 (basic booster and some simple convertibles)

**Q: Clarification: how much is a new oem car seat?**

**A:** A new OEM vehicle seat (the actual driver/passenger seat from the automaker, not a child seat) is usually in the four-figure range per seat once you include all components.

Typical price bands, per front seat, parts-only, new OEM:

* Manual cloth seat (no airbags in the seat, few electronics):
  ≈ $500–$1,200

* Power seat with airbags and heaters (typical modern sedan/SUV):
  ≈ $1,200–$2,500

* High-end / luxury seat (multi-way power, heating, cooling, memory, airbags, sometimes massage):
  ≈ $2,000–$4,500+

Notes:

* Automakers often sell the seat in major subassemblies: frame, track, cushion, cover, modules, and separate side airbag. Buying “the whole seat” typically means ordering several OEM part numbers, which pushes cost up.
* Labor adds several hundred dollars if the shop must swap airbags, wiring, sensors, and code/calibrate occupancy/airbag modules.
* Salvage yard or take-off OEM seats are often 50–80% cheaper than new.

**Q: How much for the oem fabric / seat cover mat'l?**

**A:** A new OEM-style cloth seat cover (the actual upholstery skin, not slip-on covers) is typically:

* Per bottom or backrest piece (common trucks/SUVs, cloth): about $80–$300 per section from OEM or OEM-spec replacement suppliers.
* Full front-seat OEM cover set (bottom + backrest, sometimes headrest/bolster pieces) from the automaker: roughly $250–$700+ per seat, depending on brand and trim.

Automakers usually do not sell raw yardage of seat fabric; they sell pre-cut, sewn covers by position (driver bottom, passenger back, etc.). If you only need small repairs, upholstery shops sometimes source visually matching cloth by the yard from aftermarket mills, typically around $30–$80/yard for automotive-grade fabric, but exact OEM pattern matches are rare and usually require buying the full cover.

If you give year/make/model, trim, and which section (driver bottom, passenger back, etc.), I can estimate a tighter range and suggest whether OEM vs OEM-style replacement is more economical.

**Q: Is self-install possible?**

**A:** Yes, but it is nontrivial and has safety implications.

Summary:

* Mechanically: A skilled DIYer can remove and reinstall a seat back cover on a modern GM truck/SUV seat using hand tools.
* Safety/complexity: The front passenger seat back typically contains side airbag, wiring, and occupant detection components. Mishandling can affect airbag deployment or trigger warnings.

Key points:

1. What’s involved

   * Remove seat from vehicle (often required for comfortable access).
   * Disconnect 12 V battery and wait (to depower airbag circuits).
   * Unplug seat connectors under the seat (airbag, power, occupancy/harnesses).
   * Remove plastic trim, headrest, back panel, and airbag fasteners.
   * Release J-channels, hog rings, or Velcro that secure the cover to the foam.
   * Carefully peel the old cover off, routing around airbag and wiring.
   * Install new cover, reattaching listings/hog rings so the foam isn’t loose.
   * Reinstall the airbag module, trim, and reconnect wiring.
   * Reconnect battery, check for airbag/seat/occupant warning lights.

2. Where DIY is risky

   * Side airbag integration:

     * The cover has specific seams and tear-away stitching for controlled airbag deployment.
     * The airbag module is usually bolted into the seat back frame and routed through the cover.
     * Incorrect routing, pinched fabric, or wrong cover can interfere with airbag deployment.
   * Occupant/seat sensors:

     * Misrouting or damaging sensors/wiring can cause SRS (Supplemental Restraint System) faults or disable airbags.
   * Torques and fasteners:

     * Airbag modules and seat-to-floor bolts require specific torque; undertightening/ overtightening is a safety risk.

3. When DIY is reasonable

   * You are comfortable:

     * Removing seats and trim without damaging plastics.
     * Working around airbag connectors after depowering the system.
     * Using service information (GM SI / factory manual) with torque specs and diagrams.
   * You have:

     * The correct OEM cover for the exact seat (airbag-compatible).
     * Basic tools plus hog-ring pliers (if applicable), trim tools, torque wrench.

4. When to use a pro

   * If you are not used to SRS/airbag work.
   * If you don’t have access to torque specs or a way to check/clear SRS codes.
   * Many owners buy the OEM cover but pay an upholstery shop or dealer (1–2 hours labor typical) to install it, especially on an airbag-equipped passenger seat.

Practical recommendation:

* Front passenger seat back on a GMC Sierra EV: consider this “advanced DIY.”
* If you proceed yourself:

  * Disconnect the 12 V system and follow GM service procedures step by step.
  * Use the factory manual (or equivalent) for that exact model/trim.
* If there is any doubt about correctly handling the airbag module or sensors, it is safer to have an upholstery shop or GM dealer do the install.

**Q: Are there vehicle warranty aspects to consider? If not, can I have a dealer clear the SRS codes?**

**A:** Short answer:

1. Yes, there are warranty aspects to consider, but simply replacing an OEM seat cover does not automatically void the vehicle warranty.
2. Yes, a dealer can clear SRS codes, but if they believe the fault was caused by improper work, they may bill you for diagnosis/repair.

Details:

1. Warranty framework (Magnuson–Moss, OEM warranties)

* In the U.S., the manufacturer cannot void your entire vehicle warranty just because you did your own repair or used non-dealer labor.
* However, if your work causes a failure (e.g., misrouted airbag, damaged harness), the manufacturer can deny warranty coverage for that specific failure.
* Installing an OEM seat back cover on the passenger seat becomes sensitive because it is directly tied to:

  * Side airbag deployment.
  * Occupant classification sensors.
  * Seat belt pretensioner wiring in some designs.
* If an airbag fault, sensor error, or deployment issue is traced to a DIY seat repair, that particular SRS repair would likely not be covered under warranty and would be chargeable.

2. What is “safe” from a warranty perspective

* Using the correct OEM cover for your exact VIN/trim is better than an aftermarket cover from a warranty standpoint; it matches tear seams and pattern.
* If the installation is done to factory procedure (no damaged parts, correct routing/torques, no SRS faults), the dealer has little basis to claim a warranty impact.
* Documentation helps: keep the parts invoice and any photos of harness/airbag routing during reassembly in case of later dispute.

3. Dealer clearing SRS codes

* Yes, a dealer can read and clear SRS codes with their scan tool.
* If you bring in a vehicle with an airbag light after a DIY seat repair, expect:

  * A diagnostic fee (often 0.5–1.0 hr labor).
  * If the cause is a loose connector or a minor issue, they may clear it after correcting; it is usually billable unless they consider it a goodwill courtesy.
  * If they find damaged components (sensor, harness, airbag module, or cover installed incorrectly), they will typically quote parts + labor; this would normally not be covered if they attribute it to the repair work.

4. Practical risk management

* Before you start:

  * Review the warranty booklet for language about “modifications/repairs affecting safety systems.”
  * Decide if you are comfortable being financially responsible for any SRS fault or airbag repair on that seat.
* While working:

  * Disconnect 12 V and follow the GM seat/airbag removal procedure.
  * Avoid stressing connectors and harnesses; verify everything is fully seated on reassembly.
* After reassembly:

  * If no SRS light appears after key-on/ready, you likely do not need dealer involvement.
  * If an SRS light appears and persists, do not ignore it; have it diagnosed promptly. Driving long-term with a lit airbag lamp can affect crash safety and can complicate future warranty discussions.

5. Conservative recommendation

* Because it is an EV with integrated SRS in the seat and because it is under factory warranty, the lowest-risk route is:

  * Buy the OEM cover yourself.
  * Have the dealer or a reputable upholstery shop with SRS experience install it.
* If you still prefer DIY, assume:

  * Warranty remains in force overall.
  * You may be out-of-pocket for any SRS-related corrections that can be linked to your work.
  * The dealer can clear SRS codes, but will normally charge for diagnostics and any remedial work.
