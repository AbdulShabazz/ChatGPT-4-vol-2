**Q: Why do some cinnamon products contain traces of lead?**

**A:** Some cinnamon products contain lead because lead can enter the supply chain from (1) environmental uptake, (2) processing/handling contamination, or (3) illegal adulteration—where (3) is the main driver when *elevated* lead is found.

* Environmental uptake (low-level “background” lead)
  Cinnamon is tree bark; if trees are grown in lead-contaminated soil/dust, small amounts can accumulate in the raw material. ([University at Buffalo][1])

* Processing, handling, and packaging contamination
  Lead can be introduced after harvest via contaminated water/plumbing, lead-containing equipment/solder, dust in facilities, or packaging-related sources. ([FAOHome][2])

* Illegal adulteration to enhance color/weight (common cause of high readings)
  Some suppliers have illegally added lead compounds (notably lead chromate, which contains both lead and chromium) to make spices look more vivid and/or heavier. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) reported lead and chromium in cinnamon consistent with lead chromate in the cinnamon-linked applesauce incident, and FDA’s subsequent market testing found multiple ground-cinnamon products with elevated lead. ([U.S. Food and Drug Administration][3])

**Q: So lead from additives or soil not airborne solvent?**

**A:** Correct: the dominant mechanisms are (1) intentional additives/adulterants and (2) soil/dust contamination during cultivation and handling, not an “airborne solvent” pathway.

* Lead is not meaningfully volatile at ambient conditions, so it does not travel as a gas-phase “solvent vapor” contaminant.
* “Airborne” is still relevant, but as particulate dust (soil/road/industrial dust) that deposits on crops or contaminates material during drying, grinding, storage, and packaging.
* Solvents only matter indirectly if a process water/solvent stream is itself contaminated (e.g., leaching from old plumbing), but that is a contamination source, not airborne solvent transport.

**Q: Where is cinnamon harvested from geologically?**

**A:** Most commercial “cinnamon” is harvested in four producing belts—each with characteristic climate/soil settings—rather than from a single distinctive rock type.

1. Sri Lanka (Ceylon/“true” cinnamon; *Cinnamomum verum*)

* Where: low-country wet zone, especially the southwest/coastal belt (e.g., Negombo→Matara; also inland areas such as Ratnapura). ([Department of Examinations][1])
* Geological/soil context: strongly weathered tropical soils; Sri Lanka’s cinnamon authority describes cultivation primarily on “red soils” in the wet zone. ([Cinnamon][2])

2. Indonesia (Padang/Korintje cassia; *Cinnamomum burmannii*)

* Where: especially Sumatra (notably West Sumatra/Padang and the Kerinci area), plus other islands. ([Cabi Digital Library][3])
* Geological/soil context: much of the key production is on the Indonesian volcanic arc; agronomic references note best growth in the Padang production area at ~500–1500 m elevation. ([Cabi Digital Library][3])

3. Vietnam (cassia and “Saigon” types; mainly *Cinnamomum cassia* and *C. loureiroi*)

* Where: northern highlands (e.g., Yên Bái; Lào Cai) and central provinces (e.g., Quảng Nam/Quảng Ngãi). ([McGill Geography][4])
* Geological/terrain context: described in policy/industry literature as predominantly mountainous highland cultivation zones (practically: humid, steep uplands with highly weathered soils). ([Tenure Security][5])

4. China (Chinese cassia; *Cinnamomum cassia*)

* Where: southern China, with major cultivation in Guangxi and Guangdong; broader distribution includes provinces such as Yunnan and Fujian. ([PMC][6])
* Geological/terrain context: subtropical monsoon hill/mountain agriculture on deeply weathered soils across multiple bedrock types (not a single “cinnamon geology”).

Scale note: Food and Agriculture Organization Corporate Statistical Database (FAOSTAT) reporting for 2023 indicates global production is dominated by China, Vietnam, Indonesia, and Sri Lanka. ([Wikipedia][7])

Lead-relevance (linking to your prior question): “geology” (natural soil/bedrock lead) can contribute low background levels, but the highest lead findings in cinnamon products are more consistent with supply-chain contamination or adulteration than with any specific producing region’s bedrock.
