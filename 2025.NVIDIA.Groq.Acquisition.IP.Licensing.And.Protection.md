**Q: If a public company made a license agreement with the executives of a private company to rebuild its technology in house (essentially hiring away its executive board!), can proper IP licensing requirements done by the private company restrict this, preserve its equity should it one day decide to go public?**

**A:** Yes—properly structured intellectual property (IP) ownership and licensing can significantly restrict this and preserve the private company’s equity and future public value, but only if implemented *before* the executives are hired away.

Below is the minimal legal logic.

---

### Core result

A public company cannot lawfully “rebuild” proprietary technology in-house via hired executives **without exposure** if the private company has retained IP ownership, limited licenses, and imposed enforceable confidentiality and assignment controls.

---

### What actually protects the private company

#### 1. **Clear IP ownership (non-assignable, company-owned)**

* All source code, designs, trade secrets, architectures, models, and documentation must be:

  * Owned by the private company, not individuals.
  * Explicitly excluded from executive personal IP.
* Executive agreements must include:

  * Present-tense IP assignment (“hereby assigns,” not “will assign”).
  * Survival clauses after termination.

**Effect:** Executives cannot legally re-create or transfer protected IP, even if they understand it.

---

#### 2. **Narrow, revocable, non-transferable license**

If the public company is licensed at all:

* License should be:

  * Non-exclusive
  * Non-sublicensable
  * Non-assignable
  * Field-of-use restricted
  * No right to derivative works
* Explicit prohibition on “re-implementation,” “functional replication,” or “clean-room recreation” using insider knowledge.

**Effect:** Hiring executives does not expand license scope.

---

#### 3. **Trade secret preservation**

Trade secret law protects *knowledge use*, not just copying.
Requirements:

* Documented secrecy measures.
* Access controls.
* Confidentiality agreements tied to specific technical domains.

**Effect:** Even “rebuilding from memory” can be actionable misappropriation.

---

#### 4. **No implied license**

Contracts must explicitly disclaim:

* Implied licenses
* Rights arising from access, collaboration, or employment

**Effect:** Prevents the argument that executive familiarity creates usage rights.

---

#### 5. **Equity preservation for future IPO**

If structured correctly:

* The private company retains:

  * Exclusive IP ownership
  * Clean capitalization table
  * Licenses disclosed as revenue contracts, not asset divestitures
* During an IPO, this supports:

  * Higher valuation
  * Lower IP risk discount
  * Cleaner S-1 disclosures

---

### What *does not* work

* Relying on non-compete clauses alone (weak or unenforceable in many jurisdictions).
* Informal “gentlemen’s agreements.”
* Letting executives personally own key patents or repositories.
* Broad licenses granted “for business purposes.”

---

### Bottom line

**Yes—IP licensing and ownership can prevent a public company from lawfully absorbing the technology by hiring executives, and can preserve the private company’s equity and IPO viability—but only if the IP structure is airtight *before* the executives leave.**

* Discriminatory access to drivers, libraries, or performance features.
* Strategic degradation of interoperability.

Enforcers:

* U.S. Department of Justice
* Federal Trade Commission

Limitation:
Being better, faster, or earlier is not illegal.

If designated a **gatekeeper**, a firm must:

* Ensure interoperability
* Avoid self-preferencing

* “CUDA-first” behavior could be examined as self-preferencing.

Constraint:

* Applies primarily in the EU.

## When “knowledge absorption” *does* become a violation

It crosses the line **only if all three elements are met**:

### A. The knowledge is a **trade secret**

It must be:

* Non-public
* Economically valuable *because* it is secret
* Subject to demonstrable secrecy controls

Examples that *can* qualify:

* Specific inference scheduling algorithms
* Concrete memory-layout strategies
* Exact compiler transformations
* Quantified internal benchmarks tied to design decisions

---

### B. The knowledge was acquired under a **duty of confidence**

Such as:

* NDA
* Restricted-access collaboration
* License agreement with confidentiality clauses

General market interaction or customer feedback **does not qualify**.

---

### C. The knowledge was **used or disclosed**

This is the hardest part to prove.

You must show:

* NVIDIA’s implementation is *substantially derived* from Groq’s confidential material
* Not independently developed
* Not obvious to a skilled engineer

This typically requires:

* Discovery of internal documents
* Timeline correlation
* Technical similarity analysis

Absent this, courts presume **independent development**.

What matters is **how the knowledge is embodied and used**, not **where it was acquired**.

Courts have been explicit on this point.

---

## Why company-acquired mental models remain unprotected

### Trade secret law protects **artifacts**, not cognition

Protected:

* Source code
* Schematics
* Algorithms with specific steps
* Parameter values
* Internal documents
* Concrete workflows

Not protected:

* Internalized reasoning
* Architectural intuition
* Optimization priorities
* “What works” understanding
* Causal models formed through experience

Even if:

* Learned exclusively at Groq
* Learned under NDA
* Learned while paid as an executive
* Learned through confidential failures and iteration

Once the knowledge exists **only in the mind**, it is **not a trade secret** unless it can be shown to be **used as a secret recipe**.

---

## Why NDAs cannot fix this

NDAs can prohibit:

* Disclosure of confidential materials
* Use of specific confidential information

They **cannot** prohibit:

* Applying experience
* Reusing judgment
* Reconstructing systems independently
* Generalizing lessons learned

If they tried, they would function as **de facto non-competes** and be struck down or narrowed.

---

## The key legal test (used by courts)

When Groq alleges misuse, the court asks:

> “Could a skilled engineer, with this executive’s experience, have built NVIDIA’s system **without using Groq’s confidential materials**?”

If the answer is **yes**, Groq loses—even if the executive’s insight came entirely from Groq.

This presumption strongly favors the hiring firm.

## Why this feels unjust in modern AI hardware

You are reacting correctly to a **structural mismatch**:

* Modern advantage = systems thinking + prioritization
* Law protects = discrete expressions and implementations

The law evolved for factories and blueprints, not **learning curves and inference economics**.

But the doctrine is settled.

## Bottom line

**Mental models formed at Groq are legally portable human capital, not Groq IP.**
They become Groq’s IP **only if** they are:

1. Fixed in protected form, and
2. Used in traceable, non-independent ways.

Absent that, NVIDIA may lawfully internalize the *way of thinking*—even if it took Groq years and millions to develop.
