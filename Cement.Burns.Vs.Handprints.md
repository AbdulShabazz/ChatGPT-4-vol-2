**Q: How can someone suffer 3rd degree lye burns to their flesh from liquid cement/concrete? I see celebrities leaving their palm prints in concrete!**

**A:** **Result:** Wet cement can cause **third-degree chemical burns** because it is **strongly alkaline** and can remain in prolonged contact with skin, while celebrity handprints are made under **controlled, brief, protected conditions**.

**Mechanism**

* **Portland cement + water → calcium hydroxide** (pH ~12–13).
* High alkalinity causes **liquefaction necrosis**: it dissolves fats and denatures proteins, allowing the chemical to penetrate deeper rather than forming a protective scab (unlike many acids).
* Burns are often **painless at first**, delaying removal and washing; damage continues for hours.
* Occlusion (boots, gloves, clothing) traps wet cement, increasing exposure time and severity.

**Why severe injuries occur**

* Prolonged skin contact (kneeling, boots filled with slurry, cement-splashed clothing).
* Abrasion or micro-cuts that accelerate penetration.
* Heat from hydration reactions can add minor thermal injury, compounding damage.
* Delayed decontamination due to low initial pain.

**Why celebrity handprints are safe**

* **Very short contact time** (seconds).
* **Barrier measures**: release agents (oils), liners, or surface films.
* **Low moisture** surface (stiffer mix; less free alkali at the skin).
* Immediate **washing** afterward.
* On-site supervision aware of chemical burn risk.

**Key takeaway**

* Cement burns are chemical, not thermal; **time and alkalinity** drive severity.
* Brief, protected contact is safe; **unprotected, prolonged exposure** can destroy tissue down to muscle and bone.

**Q: Is mentioned on the packaging?!**

**A:** **Yes.** Cement and concrete packaging in the United States explicitly warn about **chemical burns** and **serious skin injury**, but the language is often overlooked.

**Typical packaging language (Portland cement / premix bags):**

* “**DANGER: Causes severe skin burns and eye damage**.”
* “**Wet cement can cause serious chemical burns**.”
* “Avoid contact with skin. **Wash thoroughly after handling**.”
* “Wear **impermeable gloves, boots, long sleeves, and eye protection**.”
* “Seek medical attention for **persistent irritation or burns**.”

**Why people miss it**

* Warnings are printed in **small blocks of text** among dust/respiratory hazard notices.
* Many assume cement is a **mechanical irritant**, not a caustic chemical.
* Burns are **delayed and initially painless**, contradicting intuition.
* Cultural normalization: construction exposure is seen as routine.

**Regulatory basis**

* Classified under OSHA Hazard Communication as **corrosive when wet** (high pH from calcium hydroxide formation).
* Safety Data Sheets (SDS) universally document **alkaline chemical burns** with prolonged contact.

**Bottom line**

* The risk **is disclosed**, but not salient.
* Cement burns persist because the hazard is **counterintuitive** and **slow-acting**, not because it is undisclosed.
